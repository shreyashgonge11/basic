A. Historically Accomplished Women

Rajmata Jijau

Rajmata Jijau was a key figure in the early Maratha Empire, renowned for her political acumen and strategic foresight. As the mother of Shivaji Maharaj, she was a significant influence in shaping his vision and leadership. Jijau played an essential role in the Maratha state’s formation by providing crucial guidance and support to her son, both politically and militarily. She was known for her effective management and strategic advice, which helped Shivaji Maharaj establish a strong foundation for the Maratha Empire. Jijau’s leadership extended beyond the personal sphere, as she contributed to the empire's expansion and consolidation through her wisdom and organizational skills. Her influence was instrumental in the early successes of Shivaji Maharaj’s campaigns. Jijau’s support was crucial during the formative years of Shivaji's reign, and her contributions were pivotal in ensuring the stability and growth of the Maratha state. Her legacy is marked by her role as a mentor and strategist, whose guidance helped shape the future of the Maratha Empire. Her impact on Maratha politics and governance was profound, making her a revered figure in Indian history.

Ahilyabai Holkar

Ahilyabai Holkar (Marathi pronunciation 31 May 1725 - 13 August 1795), also spelled Ahalya Bai, was the Rajamata and later the ruling queen of Indore within the Maratha Confederacy. She established Maheshwar (in Madhya Pradesh) as the seat of the Holkar Dynasty. A beloved figure of Indian history, she is renowned for good governance, social welfare, and humanitarian work along with religious, educational, and cultural advancements. She contributed to the growth of Indian architecture through the commission of various temples, Ghats, and Dharmshalas. Ahilyabai's Matha, or charitable endowments, spread across India. Remembered as a Sadhvi, or holy woman, Ahilyabai did not grieve for the hardships she faced in life, including the losses of her husband and two of her children. She had a reputation for patience, faith, and advocacy for the elevation of Indian culture.

Rani Laxmi Bai

Rani Lakshmi Bai, the Queen of Jhansi, was a prominent leader during the Indian Rebellion of 1857 against British rule. Born in 1828, she became the queen of Jhansi through her marriage to Maharaja Gangadhar Rao. Following her husband's death in 1853, the British East India Company annexed Jhansi, triggering her fierce resistance. Lakshmi Bai emerged as a courageous and skilled military leader, leading her troops in a prolonged and valiant defense of her kingdom. Despite facing overwhelming British forces, she demonstrated exceptional bravery and strategic prowess. Her leadership inspired many, and her resistance became a symbol of Indian defiance against colonial rule. After Jhansi fell to the British in 1858, she continued to fight, eventually dying in battle. Rani Lakshmi Bai is celebrated as a national hero in India for her unyielding courage and commitment to the fight for freedom. Her legacy remains a powerful emblem of resistance and patriotism.

Madam Cama

Madam Bhikaji Cama was a prominent Indian freedom fighter and one of the earliest advocates for India's independence. During the 1907 International Socialist Congress in Stuttgart, Germany, Madam Bhikaji Cama unfurled the first version of the Indian national flag, which was designed by her. This flag was a tricolor of green, yellow, and red, with a symbol of a lotus and a crescent on it. This event was a significant moment in India's struggle for independence, as it showcased a symbol of Indian nationalism on an international stage. The flag represented the Indian national movement and was an early attempt to unify the diverse regions of India under a single symbol of independence and pride. Although this design was not adopted officially, it laid the groundwork for future developments in the design of the Indian flag. Madam Bhikaji Cama's work was instrumental in fostering a sense of national identity and pride among Indians and played a crucial role in the country's journey towards independence.

B. Accomplished Women in field of Education

Savitribai Phule

Savitribai Phule was a pioneering social reformer and educator in 19th-century India, making significant strides in improving the status of women and lower-caste communities. Along with her husband, Jyotirao Phule, she established the first school for girls in Pune in 1848, challenging societal norms and advocating for women's education. Her work extended beyond education, as she actively campaigned against caste discrimination, child marriage, and social injustices. Savitribai was also a prolific writer, using her poetry and literature to address social issues and highlight the struggles faced by women. Her support for the Satyashodhak Samaj, founded by her husband, aimed to eradicate social injustices and promote equality. Her efforts were instrumental in laying the groundwork for modern education for women and social reform in India.

Rukhmabai Raut

Rukhmabai Raut was one of the first practicing female doctors in India and a pioneering figure in the fight against child marriage and women's rights. Born in 1864, she was married off at the age of 11 but refused to live with her husband, leading to a landmark legal battle in the 1880s, known as the Rukhmabai case. This case sparked widespread debate on child marriage and women's autonomy in British India. Later, she pursued medical education in England, becoming a qualified doctor in 1894. Upon her return to India, Rukhmabai served as a physician in Surat and Rajkot, dedicating her life to women's healthcare and social reform. Her work laid the foundation for future generations of Indian women in medicine and activism.
