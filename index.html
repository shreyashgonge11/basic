A. Historically Accomplished Women

Rajmata Jijau

Rajmata Jijau was a key figure in the early Maratha Empire, renowned for her political acumen and strategic foresight. As the mother of Shivaji Maharaj, she was a significant influence in shaping his vision and leadership. Jijau played an essential role in the Maratha state’s formation by providing crucial guidance and support to her son, both politically and militarily. She was known for her effective management and strategic advice, which helped Shivaji Maharaj establish a strong foundation for the Maratha Empire. Jijau’s leadership extended beyond the personal sphere, as she contributed to the empire's expansion and consolidation through her wisdom and organizational skills. Her influence was instrumental in the early successes of Shivaji Maharaj’s campaigns. Jijau’s support was crucial during the formative years of Shivaji's reign, and her contributions were pivotal in ensuring the stability and growth of the Maratha state. Her legacy is marked by her role as a mentor and strategist, whose guidance helped shape the future of the Maratha Empire. Her impact on Maratha politics and governance was profound, making her a revered figure in Indian history.

Ahilyabai Holkar

Ahilyabai Holkar (Marathi pronunciation 31 May 1725 – 13 August 1795), also spelled Ahalya Bai, was the Rajamata and later the ruling queen of Indore within the Maratha Confederacy. She established Maheshwar (in Madhya Pradesh) as the seat of the Holkar Dynasty. A beloved figure of Indian history, she is renowned for good governance, social welfare, and humanitarian work along with religious, educational, and cultural advancements. She contributed to the growth of Indian architecture through the commission of various temples, Ghats, and Dharmshalas. Ahilyabai's Matha, or charitable endowments, spread across India. Remembered as a Sadhvi, or holy woman, Ahilyabai did not grieve for the hardships she faced in life, including the losses of her husband and two of her children. She had a reputation for patience, faith, and advocacy for the elevation of Indian culture.

Rani Laxmi Bai

Rani Laxmi Bai, born as Manikarnika Tambe, was the queen of the princely state of Jhansi in North India. She was one of the leading figures of the Indian Rebellion of 1857 and became a symbol of resistance to British rule. Known for her bravery and determination, she fought valiantly in battles and was a skilled military leader. Her leadership and courage have made her a lasting symbol of the fight for freedom in India. She was instrumental in the defense of Jhansi and actively participated in various battles, including the Battle of Jhansi, where she fought against British forces. Her legacy as a warrior queen and her contribution to the independence movement continue to inspire generations. Rani Laxmi Bai’s courage and unwavering commitment to her people have earned her a place of honor in Indian history. She remains an iconic figure, representing the spirit of resistance and the struggle for independence.

Mother Teresa

Mother Teresa, also known as Saint Teresa of Calcutta, was an Albanian-Indian Roman Catholic nun and missionary. She was born in Skopje, now the capital of North Macedonia, on 26 August 1910. She moved to Ireland and then to India, where she spent most of her life. In 1950, she founded the Missionaries of Charity, a Roman Catholic religious congregation that had over 4,500 nuns and was active in 133 countries by 2012. The congregation manages homes for people dying of HIV/AIDS, leprosy, and tuberculosis. They also run soup kitchens, dispensaries, mobile clinics, children's and family counseling programs, orphanages, and schools. Mother Teresa received numerous honors, including the 1962 Ramon Magsaysay Peace Prize and 1979 Nobel Peace Prize. Mother Teresa was canonized on 4 September 2016 and the anniversary of her death (5 September) is her feast day. Her work and life remain an enduring inspiration to people around the world.

Kalpana Chawla

Kalpana Chawla was an American astronaut and aerospace engineer who was the first woman of Indian origin to go to space. She first flew on Space Shuttle Columbia in 1997 as a mission specialist and primary robotic arm operator. In 2003, Chawla was one of the seven crew members who died in the Space Shuttle Columbia disaster when the spacecraft disintegrated during re-entry into Earth's atmosphere. Chawla was born in Karnal, India, but became a naturalized U.S. citizen in the 1980s after becoming a permanent resident. Chawla earned her Bachelor of Science in Aeronautical Engineering from Punjab Engineering College before moving to the United States to pursue further education. She went on to obtain a master's degree in aerospace engineering from the University of Texas at Arlington, and later earned a Ph.D. in aerospace engineering from the University of Colorado Boulder. In her career, Chawla made significant contributions to the field of aeronautics and space exploration. She was a role model for aspiring astronauts, particularly women, and her legacy continues to inspire future generations.
